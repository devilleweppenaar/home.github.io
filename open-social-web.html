Why this matters

The Problem with
Conventional Social Media

For the last two decades, most of what we call "social media" has followed the same basic model: a private company builds the platform, owns all the data, controls all the rules, and monetizes your attention. You are not the customer. You are the product.

This model has produced some genuinely remarkable things — global real-time conversation, discovery, community. But it has also produced something darker: a small number of extraordinarily powerful platforms with near-total control over public discourse, and the people who end up running them.

"What open source did for code, open social does for data."
— Dan Abramov, Open Social (overreacted.io)

The problems are structural, not just a matter of who happens to be in charge today.

🔒
You don't own your account
Your followers, your posts, your history — all of it lives in someone else's database. They can delete your account, restrict your reach, or shut down entirely. You have no recourse.
🚪
No exit, no leverage
If you disagree with a platform's direction or ownership, you can leave — but only by abandoning everything you've built. Your followers can't follow you out the door.
👁
Opaque algorithms
What you see, and who sees you, is controlled by algorithms you can't inspect, adjust, or verify. Engagement and outrage are rewarded because they maximize time-on-app.
👑
Concentrated power
As billionaires acquire major platforms, it becomes clear that decisions affecting billions of people's speech can hinge on one person's mood, incentives, or politics.
🚫
API lockdowns
Third-party developers once built rich tools and services on top of social platforms. Those APIs are now paywalled, restricted, or shut entirely — killing the ecosystem.
💰
Monetized attention
Every design decision is driven by ad revenue and engagement metrics. The result is platforms optimized to keep you anxious and scrolling, not informed or connected.

These aren't bugs that can be patched by a better CEO or a new content policy. They're built into the architecture. The only real fix is a different architecture entirely.

The Alternative

AT Protocol:
A New Foundation

The AT Protocol (Authenticated Transfer Protocol) is an open standard for building social applications — not owned by any one company, not controlled by any one person. Think of it the way you think about email: anyone can send an email to anyone else, through any provider, using any app. The AT Protocol is trying to do the same thing for social media.

It was created by Bluesky Social PBC, originally as a project incubated at Twitter, and is now being developed as an open standard. In 2025, the IETF (the same body that standardizes the internet's core protocols) began the process of standardizing AT Protocol; the working group charter was formally published in January 2026, and the ATP working group was officially formed in late March 2026 — a significant signal that this isn't a startup experiment, it's infrastructure.

As of early 2026, the network has over 43 million users, more than a thousand apps built on it, and 400,000 SDK downloads per month. It raised $100M in Series B funding in April 2025 — with backing from the Knight Foundation, which exists specifically to support press freedom and the public interest.

The Core Insight

The problem with today's social platforms isn't that they're social, or that they aggregate content, or that they use algorithms. All of that is genuinely valuable. The problem is that your identity and your data are trapped inside one company's database.

AT Protocol separates those things. Your identity — your username, your reputation, your social graph — is yours, not the platform's. Your data lives in a repository you control. Apps are readers and writers into that data, not owners of it. If an app turns bad, or gets acquired, or raises its prices, you can walk away without losing anything.

It's worth noting: AT Protocol is not a blockchain or cryptocurrency. It does not use tokens or require you to understand cryptography. It is a practical, engineer-facing protocol that borrows some ideas from peer-to-peer systems without the drawbacks that have made those systems hard to use.

Under the hood

How It Actually Works

You don't need to understand the technical details to use or appreciate the network. But here's a plain-language overview of the key pieces.

Your Identity

You have two identities on AT Protocol. Your handle is what people see: something like @alice.bsky.social or, if you own a domain, simply @alice.com. Usernames are literally just domain names — which means you can prove ownership of your identity the same way you prove ownership of a website.

Your DID (Decentralized Identifier) is a permanent, machine-readable ID that never changes even if you change your username or move to a different hosting provider. Think of the handle as a phone number that can be ported, and the DID as a permanent record that follows you everywhere.

Your Data Repository

Everything you do on an AT Protocol network — a post, a like, a follow, a profile update — gets written as a small piece of structured data (JSON) into your personal repository. A repository is your personal database. You control it. It's hosted on a Personal Data Server (PDS), which you can run yourself or entrust to a hosting provider — and switch providers any time.

Because all data is cryptographically signed by you, anyone can verify that a piece of content actually came from you, even if they received it from a relay or a third-party app rather than directly from your server.

The Public Firehose

One of the most distinctive features of AT Protocol is the public firehose — a real-time stream of every public action happening on the network. It is open by default. No API key, no waitlist, no per-call pricing. Any developer can connect and build something on top of it: search engines, custom feeds, bots, analytics, archiving tools, anything.

This is the opposite of what Twitter/X did when it shut down its free API. An open firehose means the ecosystem can grow in ways no single company could anticipate or control.

Moderation as a Service

Rather than one company deciding what everyone sees, AT Protocol separates moderation from hosting. Labelers are independent services that attach metadata to content — flagging spam, adult content, hate speech, or anything else. Users and apps can subscribe to whichever labelers they trust, and combine multiple labelers together. Communities can run their own. Researchers can build transparent ones. Companies can offer commercial ones.

This doesn't mean "anything goes" — it means moderation is a competitive service rather than a monopoly, and you have real choice in who provides it.

What's being built

The Atmosphere:
Apps & Projects

The collection of apps, services, and infrastructure built on AT Protocol is called the Atmosphere. It is growing quickly — more than a thousand apps are in active use weekly, spanning social clients, photo and video, events, writing, and community infrastructure. All of them share the same identity layer: your @handle and social graph work across every one of them.

The examples below represent a cross-section of what's being built. For a comprehensive, up-to-date directory of apps, feeds, and tools, see the Bluesky Directory.

Social networks & Clients
Flagship app
Bluesky
The most popular AT Protocol app — 43M+ users, microblogging in the Twitter tradition, but with user-owned data and open feeds. The reference implementation of what AT Protocol can do.
Video social network
Skylight
A video-first social network with a "your feed, your rules" ethos — backed by Mark Cuban, featured in TechCrunch, WIRED, and TIME. Surpassed 380,000 users in January 2026. A Public Benefit Corporation legally committed to user wellbeing over profit. Sign in with your existing Bluesky account; your followers come with you.
Alternative client
Anisota
A radical rethink of the social feed. Instead of endless scrolling, you flip through a finite deck of posts like trading cards — each with a "rarity" rating in place of engagement counts. Stamina limits discourage compulsive use. Calm, nature-themed, deliberately slow. Built by an independent artist in Appalachia.
Photo & Video
Photo sharing
Flashes
An Instagram-style photo sharing app built on AT Protocol. Hit 30,000 downloads in its first 24 hours after launching in February 2025. Supports filters, a Portfolio Mode for photographers, and curated artist feeds.
Short video
Spark
A video-first app built directly on AT Protocol — not just on Bluesky — with longer video support, filters, effects, and a music library. Featured in Forbes and TechCrunch. Currently in open beta.
Livestreaming
Streamplace
The first livestreaming platform built on AT Protocol. All content is cryptographically signed by creators. Streamplace 2.0 — backed by Livepeer — launched in 2026, adding video-on-demand and deeper ecosystem integration. Powers the official ATmosphereConf conference stream. Available on iOS and Android, and supports OBS for desktop streaming.
Community & Events
Events & RSVPs
Smoke Signal
An events and RSVP platform built on AT Protocol, written in Rust and fully open source. Organise events without surrendering your data to a platform like Eventbrite or Facebook Events.
Local communities
OpenMeet
An open-source, free alternative to Meetup.com for interest groups, community networks, and local events. Events are published as AT Protocol records — portable and user-owned. Sign in with your Bluesky account directly.
Local discovery
Localsky
A city-level social layer on top of AT Protocol — browse posts tuned for local discovery so nearby updates don't get buried in a global feed. Log in with your existing Bluesky account.
Infrastructure & Scale

The ecosystem isn't just apps. A parallel wave of infrastructure projects is making sure the network doesn't depend on Bluesky alone to keep running.

Independent relay
Free Our Feeds
An initiative — now formalised as the Modal Foundation, a Netherlands-based non-profit — building community-owned relay infrastructure so the firehose isn't solely operated by Bluesky. The goal: a network that no single company can switch off.
European infrastructure
EuroSky
A European effort to run AT Protocol infrastructure independently — ensuring the open social web isn't concentrated exclusively in US-based servers and companies. In March 2026 Eurosky announced a partnership with Igalia, the open-source consultancy, to develop core AT Protocol tools released under an open MIT licence.
Community-owned PDS
Northsky
A safer social space for 2SLGBTQIA+ communities, run as a non-profit worker-owned cooperative in Canada. Operates its own AT Protocol PDS, with active moderation and democratic governance baked in from the start.
Reference

Key Terms

AT Protocol
The open standard this whole document is about. Authenticated Transfer Protocol. The rules for how social data is stored, signed, and shared across the network.
The Atmosphere
The name for the whole ecosystem of apps, services, and infrastructure built on AT Protocol. Like "the web" for this new social layer.
DID
Decentralized Identifier. Your permanent, unchanging identity on the network — doesn't change even if you change your username or move servers.
Handle
Your human-readable username, like @alice.bsky.social or @alice.com. Based on a domain name. Can be changed; the DID stays constant underneath.
Repository
Your personal data store. All your posts, likes, follows, and profile data live here as structured records. You own it.
PDS
Personal Data Server. Hosts your repository. Can be run by you, by Bluesky, or by any provider. Can be switched at any time without data loss.
Relay / Firehose
A relay collects activity from all PDSes and rebroadcasts it as a real-time stream. The firehose is that stream — open to anyone to read, build on, or archive.
Labeler
A moderation service that attaches labels to content or accounts. You subscribe to whichever labelers you trust. Moderation is decentralized, not monopolized.
Lexicon
The schema system for AT Protocol — defines what kinds of records exist and what they contain. app.bsky.feed.post is the schema for a Bluesky post; anyone can define new schemas for new apps.
AT URI
The address format for data on the network: at://alice.com/app.bsky.feed.post/3k4da.... Like a URL but tied to identity, not location.
Federation
The ability for independent servers to interoperate. AT Protocol is a federated protocol — multiple hosts, one network — similar in spirit to email.
ActivityPub
The other major open social protocol, used by Mastodon. Takes a different architectural approach; AT Protocol's key differences are account portability, global-scale feeds, and strong identity.
